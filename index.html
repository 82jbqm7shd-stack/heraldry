Heraldry of Mark Richard Hopking

A research-led account of the arms granted to Mark Richard Hopking by the Court of the Lord Lyon in 2018.

This page examines the documented armorial traditions that informed the design of the Scottish grant, distinguishing between established evidence of descent, surname association, and structural references. Where descent is unproven, this is stated explicitly.

The Origin of a Modern Scottish Grant of Arms

The arms granted to Mark Richard Hopking by the Court of the Lord Lyon in 2018 are a modern Scottish creation, lawfully constituted and recorded on page 90 of volume 93 of the Public Register of All Arms and Bearings in Scotland.[1][2] They do not purport to be inherited arms, nor do they assert continuity of armorial right across surnames. Rather, they represent a considered synthesis of documented armorial traditions associated with several ancestral family lines, together with long-standing family usage, refined and regularised under Scottish heraldic law.

This page examines how the final blazon came about, the sources that informed it, and the distinction between established evidence and conjecture.

Scottish Heraldic Context

In Scotland, heraldry is regulated by statute and administered by the Court of the Lord Lyon. Arms are individual, heritable property and may be borne only by those to whom they have been granted or matriculated. No right to arms arises merely from surname, descent, or custom.

Accordingly, the arms discussed here exist because they were granted, not because of ancestry. Historical arms associated with surnames or families may inform the design of a new grant, but they do not in themselves confer armorial right.

The Court of the Lord Lyon determines the legality and form of arms; symbolic interpretation lies outside its judicial function.

Methodology and Sources

The design of the Hopking arms was informed by armorial precedents associated with five surnames appearing in the petitioner's ancestry:

  • Hopking / Hopkin(s)
  • Walsham
  • Scarlett
  • Fortin
  • Chivers

These sources fall into three evidential categories:

  1. Documented ancestral armigers (Walsham, Scarlett)
  2. Surname-associated arms with no proven descent (Hopking / Hopkin(s))
  3. Attested arms with no demonstrated genealogical connection (Fortin, Chivers)

Where descent is unproven, this is stated explicitly. No claim of inherited armorial right is made in such cases.

The Source Armorial Traditions

Hopking / Hopkin(s)

There is no established tradition of arms borne under the surname Hopking in Scotland.

In England, however, the surname appears historically in several variant forms—Hopkin, Hopkins, Hopkyn, and Hopkyng—which are frequently co-located in early modern records, particularly in Northamptonshire. These spellings reflect orthographic variation in historical record-keeping rather than distinct surnames in the modern sense.

The armorial material discussed below relates to the surname Hopkin as it appears in English armorial sources. It is presented for contextual and comparative purposes only and does not imply the existence of inherited, historically borne, or genealogically transmissible arms for the modern surname Hopking.

A blazon frequently associated with the surname is:

Sable, on a chevron Or, between three ancient pistols, three roses Gules.

While no genealogical descent from an English armiger bearing these arms has been demonstrated, this blazon provided the structural foundation for the Scottish grant. The field, chevron, pistols, and roses were retained as the principal elements of the shield.

This use is explicitly one of surname association, not inheritance.

Walsham

The Walsham family constitutes a fully documented armigerous ancestral line. Documentary evidence establishes that the arms borne by the 1st and 2nd Walsham Baronets were not quartered in the conventional heraldic sense, but formed a composite arrangement of nine armorial bearings displayed in three rows of three.[3][4]

Within this composite achievement, the ancient Walsham arms occupied the first position. It is this ancient Walsham element alone that is referenced on this page. No claim is made to the composite arms as a whole, nor to any broader armorial inheritance beyond that expressly incorporated into the Scottish grant.

Sable, on a cross voided Or, five crosses patée fitchée of the last.

This blazon represents the ancient Walsham arms only, as distinct from both the later composite arms borne by the Walsham baronets and the subsequent quartered arms borne by later descendants, including Mark Hopking's great-grandfather.

In the Scottish grant, the crosses patée fitchée were adopted as the principal Walsham reference. Rather than reproducing the English arms wholesale, two such crosses were placed in the chief, flanking the central charge. The shared use of a black field in both Walsham and Hopkin arms further facilitated their integration within a coherent Scottish design.

Together, these successive forms represent the historical development of a single Walsham armorial tradition. Only its ancient form is referenced here as an armorial source; later composite and quartered expressions are noted solely as genealogical and evidential context.

Scarlett

The Scarlett family represents a senior armigerous line with a well-documented and continuous heraldic tradition. Across multiple branches and successive generations—including the Jamaican branch, the peerage of Baron Abinger, and later complex quartered forms—the core Scarlett arms consistently feature a lion rampant Ermine.[5][6]

Although the Scarlett arms were subject over time to augmentation, quartering, and the addition of cantons, supporters, and external ornaments, the lion rampant Ermine remained a stable and recognisable armorial element. Even in later composite achievements, the Scarlett arms proper, bearing the lion rampant Ermine, customarily occupy the first position.

Chequy Or and Gules, a lion rampant Ermine; on a canton Azure a castle triple-towered Argent.

This blazon represents the Scarlett arms proper, abstracted from later elaborations and quarterings. No claim is made on this page to the peerage arms, supporters, coronets, or subsequent quartered achievements associated with later members of the family.

In the Scottish grant of arms to Mark Hopking, the lion rampant Ermine was adopted as the sole Scarlett reference. Its long-standing continuity across the Scarlett armorial tradition, and its ability to stand independently as a heraldic charge without importing English peerage apparatus, made it particularly suited to incorporation within a Scottish heraldic design.

The Scarlett arms are therefore treated here as a single, continuous armorial source, expressed historically in increasingly elaborate forms, but unified by the persistent use of the lion rampant Ermine as their defining charge.

Scarlett descent. The connection to the Scarlett family is through Florence Scarlett (born 20 March 1844), daughter of Peter Campbell Scarlett CB, DL, who was the son of Sir James Scarlett, 1st Baron Abinger. Florence married Sir John Walsham, 2nd Baronet, establishing the documented Walsham-Scarlett line through which descent is traced.

Fortin

The surname Fortin appears in continental armorial sources, notably in Reitstap's Armorial Général, where arms are recorded as:

D'Azure, à deux dragons d'Or.

No evidence has been established linking the petitioner genealogically to an armigerous Fortin family. Accordingly, no claim is made to Fortin arms. Their influence is confined to the use of Azure as a tincture within the Scottish design. The dragon charge was deliberately excluded.

Chivers

The Chivers arms are unusual but well documented in heraldic literature, notably as:

Argent, three cinquefoils Gules, and a giron Azure.

As with Fortin, no proven descent has been demonstrated. The Chivers influence is therefore limited to colour language rather than charge. The Azure element was absorbed into the chief as part of a broader secondary tincture scheme.

Synthesis of the Scottish Arms

The final Scottish arms bring together these influences in a manner consistent with Scottish heraldic practice, while remaining distinct and original.

Shield

The shield retains the Hopkin structural blazon: a field Sable, a chevron Or, three ancient pistols, and three roses Gules.

Upon this foundation, a chief Azure was introduced to carry the principal ancestral references:

  • A lion rampant Ermine (from Scarlett), centrally placed
  • Two crosses patée fitchée Or (from Walsham), flanking the lion

Azure serves as a secondary tincture, informed by Fortin and Chivers armorial colour usage.

The Fillet Argent

In the arms as originally petitioned, the chief Azure lay directly upon the field Sable. Under Scottish heraldic law, direct colour-on-colour adjacency between field and chief requires formal separation. At the stage of grant, the Lord Lyon introduced a fillet Argent in the lower part of the chief, thereby separating Azure from Sable and bringing the arms into full compliance with the Laws of Arms.

This was a technical heraldic refinement, not a symbolic addition, and it preserved the conceptual integrity of the design. The initial warrant was issued without the fillet, but when preparing the grant for the Herald Artist, the tincture rule violation was identified. A revised warrant incorporating the fillet Argent was issued at the last moment to resolve this technical issue.

Helm

In accordance with Scottish practice, the Letters Patent depict a helm befitting the petitioner's degree at the time of grant in 2018, when he did not yet hold a Scottish feudal barony. At that time, the appropriate helm was that of an Esquire.

Following the later acquisition of the Barony of Balvill in 2025, the armiger is thereafter entitled, by custom, to depict a helm appropriate to that dignity in subsequent artistic renderings. The original grant remains unaltered, and no inconsistency arises between the juridical record and later representational usage.

Crest and Motto

Crest

The crest—a double-turreted tower in prospect Argent, masoned Sable, flammant on a rock Proper—has been used within the family for generations, notably on signet rings and personal items. Documentary evidence of its use extends back five generations.

No documentary source has been identified for the crest's original adoption, and no claim is made to its antiquity or original grant. Its resemblance to the castle badge associated with the Suffolk Regiment is acknowledged, but no connection is asserted.

The crest is therefore presented as a traditional family emblem, formally regularised through the Scottish grant. An early family document from the nineteenth century shows a slightly different blazon, described as "A Flaming Fort" with the motto "Inter Primos" (Among the First) or "Coming the first / otherwise always in the front." This historical variant was discovered only after the 2018 grant had been made.

The granted blazon represents a modernised and technically precise description of the crest as it had evolved in family usage by the time of the petition.

Motto

The motto TANTUM OPTIMUS EST, which translates as "Only the Best", originated as a personal family expression specific to Mark Hopking, reflecting a long-standing characteristic rather than a historical motto inherited from armigerous ancestors. The phrase existed in English within the family and was Latinised for the heraldic grant.

The historical motto "Inter Primos" (Among the First), discovered on the early family crest document after the 2018 grant had been completed, represents an intriguing parallel. Both mottoes express themes of primacy and excellence, though they were adopted independently and at different times.

The granted motto TANTUM OPTIMUS EST is therefore a modern, personal expression, distinct from any historical family motto, though thematically consonant with the earlier "Inter Primos."

The Arms as Granted

The arms, crest, motto, and pennon exist as a single juridical creation of the Court of the Lord Lyon. While informed by ancestry and tradition, their authority derives solely from the grant itself as recorded on page 90 of volume 93 of the Public Register of All Arms and Bearings in Scotland.[1]
